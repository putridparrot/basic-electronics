# Resistors

A resistor is one of the most fundamental components in electronics. Almost every circuit contains at least one. Understanding what a resistor does, how it is constructed, and how to use it correctly is an essential foundation for everything else.

---

## What is a Resistor?

A resistor is a passive two-terminal component that **opposes the flow of electric current**. It does this by converting some of the electrical energy into heat — a process called *resistive dissipation*. The amount of opposition it provides is called its **resistance**, measured in **ohms (Ω)**.

The relationship between voltage, current, and resistance is described by Ohm's Law:

```
V = I × R
```

- More resistance → less current for the same voltage.
- Less resistance → more current for the same voltage.

Resistors are *passive* — they do not amplify or generate energy; they only consume it (as heat). They are also *linear* (within their rated range) — double the voltage and you get double the current.

## Core Equations

Ohm's law and power equations are the basis for resistor selection.

```
V = I x R
I = V / R
R = V / I
```

Power dissipated by a resistor:

```
P = V x I
P = I^2 x R
P = V^2 / R
```

Where:

- `V` = voltage across resistor (volts)
- `I` = current through resistor (amps)
- `R` = resistance (ohms)
- `P` = power dissipated as heat (watts)

## Units and Practical Ranges

- `1 ohm` (ohm)
- `1 kohm` = `1000 ohm`
- `1 Mohm` = `1,000,000 ohm`

Typical ranges in real circuits:

- Current sensing: `0.001 ohm` to `1 ohm`
- LED current limiting: `100 ohm` to `10 kohm`
- Logic pull-up/down: `1 kohm` to `100 kohm` (often `10 kohm`)
- Biasing/timing: `1 kohm` to `1 Mohm`

## Important Real-World Parameters

Ideal resistors only have resistance. Real parts include additional behavior.

- Tolerance: e.g. `+/-0.1%`, `+/-1%`, `+/-5%`
- Power rating: e.g. `0.063 W`, `0.125 W`, `0.25 W`, `0.5 W`, `1 W`
- Temperature coefficient (tempco): ppm per degree C, e.g. `25 ppm/C`, `100 ppm/C`
- Maximum working voltage: do not exceed, even if power is within limit
- Noise: composition and film types have different excess noise
- Pulse/surge capability: short-duration overload tolerance
- Long-term stability: drift over time and humidity

## Resistor Types and Tradeoffs

## 1) Carbon Film

- Low cost, general purpose
- Moderate tolerance and temp stability
- More noise and drift than precision metal film

Best use:

- Non-critical general circuits

## 2) Metal Film

- Good tolerance and low noise
- Better temp stability and long-term drift performance
- Common precision through-hole choice

Best use:

- Analog signal paths, precision dividers, instrumentation

## 3) Thick-Film SMD

- Very common in SMT assembly
- Good cost/performance for digital and general analog
- Available in many package sizes and power ratings

Best use:

- Most modern PCB resistor needs

## 4) Thin-Film SMD

- High precision, low tempco, low noise
- Usually more expensive than thick-film

Best use:

- Precision analog, reference networks, measurement front-ends

## 5) Wirewound

- High power capability and robust thermal behavior
- Inductance can be significant (not ideal at high frequency unless non-inductive type)

Best use:

- Power loads, current limiting, braking/dummy loads

## 6) Current Sense (Shunt)

- Very low resistance values
- Designed for accurate current measurement and Kelvin connection
- Often specified by TCR, tolerance, and power pulse rating

Best use:

- Motor drivers, power supplies, battery management

## Tolerance and Accuracy

Tolerance defines how far actual resistance may be from nominal value at reference conditions.

- `1 kohm +/-5%` may be between `950 ohm` and `1050 ohm`
- `1 kohm +/-1%` may be between `990 ohm` and `1010 ohm`

For divider, gain, and measurement circuits, tolerance often directly affects system accuracy.

## Temperature Coefficient (Tempco)

Tempco indicates resistance change with temperature.

Approximate relation:

```
Delta R = R0 x alpha x Delta T
```

Where:

- `R0` = nominal resistance
- `alpha` = tempco in fraction per degree C (for ppm/C, divide by 1,000,000)
- `Delta T` = temperature change

Example:

- `10 kohm`, `100 ppm/C`, `50 C` rise
- `Delta R = 10000 x (100/1000000) x 50 = 50 ohm` (0.5%)

## Power Rating and Derating

A resistor must safely dissipate heat generated by current.

Selection rule of thumb:

- Calculate expected dissipation.
- Choose a resistor with comfortable margin (often 2x or more in practical designs).

Important:

- Rated power is usually specified at a reference ambient temperature (often around 70 C).
- Above that temperature, allowable power is derated per datasheet curve.
- Also check voltage rating; high-value resistors can exceed voltage limits before power limits.

## Series and Parallel Combinations

Series resistors:

```
Rtotal = R1 + R2 + ...
```

Parallel resistors:

```
1/Rtotal = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + ...
```

Two-resistor parallel shortcut:

```
Rtotal = (R1 x R2) / (R1 + R2)
```

Use cases:

- Series: increase total resistance or share voltage stress
- Parallel: reduce resistance or share dissipation

## Voltage Divider (with Loading Awareness)

Unloaded divider:

```
Vout = Vin x (R2 / (R1 + R2))
```

Real circuits load the divider. Input resistance of the next stage appears in parallel with `R2`, changing output.

Practical guidance:

- Keep divider resistance low enough for load immunity, but not so low it wastes power.
- Buffer with an op-amp if precision and low source impedance are needed.

## Common Circuit Uses

## 1) LED Current Limiting

```
R = (Vsupply - Vforward) / Iled
```

Example:

- `5 V` supply, `2 V` LED, `10 mA` target
- `R = (5 - 2) / 0.01 = 300 ohm`
- Nearest preferred value often `330 ohm`

## 2) Pull-Up and Pull-Down

- Define default logic state when switch/transistor is open
- Typical values: `4.7 kohm` to `100 kohm` depending on speed/noise/power tradeoff

## 3) Bias Networks

- Set transistor or op-amp operating point
- Stability depends on tolerance/tempco and source/load interaction

## 4) Current Sensing

- Measure voltage drop across low-ohm shunt
- Use Kelvin routing for better accuracy

## 5) RC Timing and Filtering

Used with capacitors for delay and frequency shaping.

```
tau = R x C
fc = 1 / (2 x pi x R x C)
```

## Markings and Value Identification

## Through-Hole Color Code

Typical 4-band:

- Band 1: first digit
- Band 2: second digit
- Band 3: multiplier
- Band 4: tolerance

Typical 5-band:

- Bands 1-3: significant digits
- Band 4: multiplier
- Band 5: tolerance

Digit colors:

- Black 0, Brown 1, Red 2, Orange 3, Yellow 4
- Green 5, Blue 6, Violet 7, Grey 8, White 9

Common tolerance colors:

- Gold `+/-5%`, Silver `+/-10%`, Brown `+/-1%`, Red `+/-2%`

## SMD Markings

3-digit code:

- `103` = `10 x 10^3 ohm` = `10 kohm`
- `472` = `47 x 10^2 ohm` = `4.7 kohm`

`R` indicates decimal point:

- `4R7` = `4.7 ohm`
- `0R22` = `0.22 ohm`

Precision codes (EIA-96) are also common on 1% parts.

## Preferred Values (E-Series)

Resistor values are standardized in logarithmic E-series.

- E6, E12, E24: common coarse sets
- E48, E96, E192: finer precision sets

This allows predictable value spacing and availability.

## Common Failure Modes

- Overpower/overtemperature: value drift, discoloration, open circuit
- Overvoltage arc or film damage
- Mechanical stress/cracking (especially SMD on flexing PCBs)
- Moisture/environmental drift in poor conditions

## Measurement and Troubleshooting Tips

- Measure out-of-circuit for accurate value (in-circuit paths can distort reading).
- If in-circuit measurement is lower than expected, parallel paths are likely.
- Inspect for heat damage around high-power parts.
- For precision networks, verify at operating temperature, not only room temperature.

## Selection Checklist

1. Nominal resistance value.
2. Tolerance required by circuit accuracy.
3. Dissipated power and derating margin.
4. Tempco and operating temperature range.
5. Voltage rating and surge/pulse demands.
6. Technology type (noise, stability, inductance, cost).
7. Package size and assembly method (THT/SMD).

## Safety Notes

- High-power resistors run hot in normal operation; allow clearance and ventilation.
- Use flameproof or fusible resistors in safety-critical mains areas where appropriate.
- Verify creepage/clearance and voltage rating in high-voltage designs.

## Quick Reference Values

- LED on 5 V (red LED, ~10 mA): often `220 ohm` to `330 ohm`
- Logic pull-up: typically `10 kohm`
- I2C pull-up (board dependent): often `2.2 kohm` to `10 kohm`
- Op-amp input bias paths: often `10 kohm` to `1 Mohm`
